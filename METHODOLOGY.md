# ATS-CVC Framework v1.0
## Methodology & Evidence Base

> *AI is the engine that applies this framework. The framework is the product.*

---

## What This Framework Is

The ATS-CVC Framework is an evidence-based methodology for analysing the gap between how a candidate presents themselves and what a hiring manager is actually looking for in a specific role.

It is not a CV writing guide. It does not produce CVs. It identifies gaps, framing failures, and interview risks — and it places the responsibility for addressing those gaps firmly with the candidate, where it belongs.

The framework applies to any profession, any seniority level, any industry. It uses AI as an interpreter — so that the analytical principles apply whether the candidate is a nurse, a software engineer, or a security analyst — without requiring domain-specific keyword libraries for every profession.

The five analytical outputs this framework produces are documented in full in [Part 4](#part-4--the-five-analytical-outputs) of this document. That section is the core of the methodology.

## Part 1 — The Market Context

Understanding the environment your CV must navigate.

### The hiring pipeline

A typical corporate role receives 250 applications (Glassdoor average). Of those, fewer than 3% reach interview stage (CareerSidekick). Candidates are lost at each stage not through automatic rejection, but through misparse and irrelevance:

```
250 applications → ~175 pass ATS parse → ~50 human review → ~12 shortlisted → ~3 interviewed
```

In a contracting market, every application counts more. A CV that was adequate when demand outstripped supply is no longer sufficient.

### The ATS gatekeeping reality

The "75% auto-rejection by ATS" statistic — widely cited across careers advice sites — has been professionally debunked. It originated from Preptel, a company that ceased trading in 2013, and no methodology was ever disclosed (Assaf, 2013; HiringThing, 2025). The Enhancv September 2025 study of 25 recruiters found only 8% enable content auto-rejection; 92% rely on human review.

**The real ATS risk is not rejection — it is invisible misparse.**

When an ATS cannot read your CV correctly, it does not reject it — it corrupts the data. Your name may appear, but your skills, job titles, and dates may be scrambled, missing, or assigned to the wrong fields. You pass to human review with a broken profile.

The EDLIGO 2025 study of 1,000 CVs across Workday, Taleo, and Greenhouse quantified parse failure by format:

| Format | Parse failure rate |
|---|---|
| .docx single column | 4% |
| .docx two column | 14% |
| PDF single column | 18% |
| PDF two column | 28% |

**Default to .docx unless explicitly asked for PDF.**

### The recruiter screen

The TheLadders 2018 eye-tracking study (n=30 professional recruiters, 10-week period) found that 80% of initial screen time — averaging 7.4 seconds — is spent on just six data points:

1. Name
2. Current title
3. Current employer
4. Current dates
5. Previous title
6. Previous employer

Every structural decision in this framework is designed to make those six data points instantly legible.

If your CV passes the initial screen, the average full review time is 2 minutes 17 seconds (CV Genius UK 2024, n=625). That is the window in which your experience, skills, and positioning must make the case.

### The AI detection problem

74% of UK hiring managers claim they can identify AI-generated CV content (CV Genius survey, April 2024, n=625). 57% said they were significantly less likely to hire a candidate whose CV appeared AI-generated, and would dismiss the application instantly.

The tell is not AI assistance per se — it is the language patterns that AI defaults to when left without constraints. The following phrases appear in AI-generated CVs at a rate that has trained hiring managers to recognise them:

> *delve, streamline, foster, testament to, underscore, facilitate, pivotal, showcasing*

These words are not wrong. They are just so common in AI output that they read as a signal. Remove them before submitting any application.

**The implication for this framework:** AI is used to analyse and identify gaps — not to generate the language of your CV. The words on your CV should be yours.

## Part 2 — The Four Guardrails

Every analytical output this framework produces operates under four non-negotiable constraints. They exist because the most common failure mode of AI-assisted CV work is fabrication dressed as improvement.

**These are not optional.** CV fraud is a criminal offence under the Fraud Act 2006, carrying up to 10 years' imprisonment. In *R v Andrewes* [2022] UKSC 24, the Supreme Court upheld a confiscation order of £96,737 against an NHS executive who falsified qualifications. The guardrails throughout this framework are non-negotiable.

---

### Guardrail 1 — No invented quantification

Never suggest, invent, estimate, or imply a metric not directly evidenced in the content provided. If no number exists, write the bullet without one. Do not suggest ranges, approximations, or placeholders such as *X%* unless explicitly instructed by the candidate.

**The risk this prevents:** Numbers that sound plausible but were never real — percentages, values, team sizes, counts — that collapse the moment an interviewer asks how you arrived at them.

---

### Guardrail 2 — No verb upgrades beyond accuracy

Do not upgrade action verbs beyond what the evidence supports. The distinction between *Led* and *Contributed to*, *Designed* and *Operated*, *Built* and *Managed* is a claim of ownership — not a stylistic choice.

**The risk this prevents:** Language that implies more ownership, initiative, or seniority than the role genuinely involved — overclaimed in a way that surfaces immediately in technical or competency-based questioning.

---

### Guardrail 3 — No unevidenced skill assumptions

Do not add skills, technologies, frameworks, or responsibilities that have not been explicitly mentioned in the candidate's CV. If a JD requirement has no matching evidence, flag it as a gap rather than inventing a partial match.

**The risk this prevents:** Additions that seem reasonable for the role but were never the candidate's — which become a problem when the interviewer asks them to go deeper on something that isn't actually there.

---

### Guardrail 4 — Flag every assumption explicitly

Whenever an inference, reframing decision, or positioning choice is not explicitly stated in the source material, surface it clearly. Do not embed assumptions in fluent prose where they might be missed.

**The risk this prevents:** Inferences the candidate doesn't notice until they are sitting in the interview room and cannot substantiate what the CV claims.

**This is the most important guardrail.** An AI that quietly makes a reasonable-sounding assumption is harder to catch than one that refuses to proceed — because the output reads as familiar and the gap is invisible until it matters. Explicit flags turn every uncertain decision into a visible question that can be answered before the CV leaves the candidate's hands.

## Part 3 — The Eight Stages

The framework operates as an eight-stage sequential process. The stages are cumulative — each depends on the previous. Skipping stages produces a CV that looks tailored but is not.

For the purposes of the ATS-CVC tool, these stages are applied analytically rather than interactively. The tool runs the equivalent of Stages 2, 3, and 7 in a single pass, producing the five structured outputs documented in Part 4.

For candidates who wish to run the full framework manually — to actually rewrite their CV against a JD — the stage-by-stage process is documented below.

---

### Stage 0 — AI Guardrails

Confirm all four guardrails with the AI before any work begins. The AI should confirm each constraint by number before proceeding. Do not begin any stage until confirmation is clear.

---

### Stage 1 — Pre-Work & Positioning

Define what the CV needs to say before any words change.

A CV for a senior individual contributor role and a CV for a people management role may contain identical experience — but the emphasis, section order, and profile will differ entirely. This stage establishes the strategic frame before any rewriting begins.

**Five questions to answer before running Stage 1:**

1. What is the target role type? Be specific about function and level — not just job title.
2. What is your single strongest credential for this role? The one thing that makes a hiring manager want to read further.
3. What is the honest framing? Are you applying to a role that matches your current position, or are you changing direction, stepping up, or returning to a different kind of work?
4. Are there gaps or transitions to manage? Career gaps, role changes, missing qualifications, sector moves. Not hiding them — managing them.
5. What is the application route? Directly through an employer's system, via a recruiter, or through a personal referral. The route affects how much weight keyword matching carries.

---

### Stage 2 — JD Deconstruction

Extract and rank the keywords and requirements that actually matter.

Job descriptions are written by multiple people with different priorities. They are rarely clean specifications of what the role requires. This stage produces a ranked keyword list and a plain-English deconstruction of what the role genuinely needs.

**How to read a job description:**

- **Essential vs desirable:** 'Must have' and 'essential' are filter criteria — non-negotiable. 'Desirable' and 'advantageous' are differentiators, not requirements.
- **Frequency signals weight:** A term that appears in multiple sections of the same JD carries more weight than one that appears once.
- **The first three responsibility bullets describe the real job:** Everything after is secondary.

---

### Stage 3 — Gap Analysis

Map every JD requirement against the candidate's evidenced experience.

Classify each requirement into one of five categories:

| Classification | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **STRONG** | Clearly evidenced, correctly framed, no action needed |
| **REWRITE** | Evidenced but the bullet undersells it — needs a stronger frame |
| **REFRAME** | Evidenced but described with different terminology to the JD |
| **BRIDGE** | Partially evidenced — adjacent experience that needs positioning |
| **GAP** | Not evidenced and cannot be bridged honestly |

**Decision rule:** If two or more essential requirements are genuine GAPs with no credible bridge, a well-crafted CV will not change the outcome at interview. Apply with realistic expectations or reconsider before investing a full rewrite.

---

### Stage 4 — Profile Rewrite

A four-sentence profile built specifically for this role.

The profile is the first thing a recruiter reads. It must immediately signal: who you are, your strongest credential for this role, the specific value you bring, and the level you operate at. It must not be generic.

**Specificity test:** Would this profile, word for word, work on someone else's CV for the same role? If yes, it is not specific enough.

**Phrases to remove before finishing:**

> *results-driven, passionate about, highly motivated, experienced professional, proven track record, dynamic, innovative, strategic thinker*

These phrases say nothing. Replace every one with a concrete claim.

---

### Stage 5A — Bullet Interrogation

Challenge each bullet before rewriting. Five questions per bullet:

1. Did I do this, or did I contribute to someone else doing it?
2. Did I design it, or implement a design I was given?
3. Was this my initiative, or a task I was directed to do?
4. Can I prove this claim in a 10-minute interview?
5. Does this bullet describe a task or an outcome?

A bullet that cannot survive these five questions should not be on the CV, regardless of how well it reads.

---

### Stage 5B — Bulk Bullet Rewrite

With the guardrails confirmed and the interrogation complete, rewrite bullets in batch. Every rewrite must:

- Open with the most confident **accurate** verb
- Contain a measurable or observable outcome where one genuinely exists
- Use the JD's own terminology where the candidate's experience supports it
- Carry no flag from Stage 5A that has not been resolved

---

### Stage 6 — Skills Architecture

Build a grouped skills section that mirrors how the hiring manager thinks.

Every skill listed must be evidenced in an experience bullet. A skills section that lists things not mentioned in the experience section is a credibility risk — interviewers probe skills sections first.

Group skills by category using the JD's own structure where possible. If the JD has sections for 'Technical Skills', 'Security Tools', and 'Frameworks', mirror those groupings.

---

### Stage 7 — Final Audit

Three lenses on the finished CV before submission:

**Lens 1 — ATS parse check.** Single column, no tables, no text boxes, no images, no header/footer content. Contact details in the body of the document. Acronyms expanded on first use.

**Lens 2 — Six-second scan test.** Can a recruiter identify name, current title, current employer, current dates, previous title, and previous employer in under ten seconds? If not, fix the layout before anything else.

**Lens 3 — Hiring manager perspective.** Read every bullet as someone who has interviewed candidates for this role before. Flag any claim that would prompt a probing question you cannot fully answer.

**Submit decision rule:** If Stage 7 identifies multiple critical changes, make them before submitting. A delayed application is recoverable. A rejected one is not.

## Part 4 — The Five Analytical Outputs

This is the core of the ATS-CVC Framework. The five outputs are what the tool produces, and they reflect a specific analytical position about what actually determines whether a candidate gets an interview.

The five outputs are not a summary of the CV. They are an analysis of the gap between the CV and the role — and of how a hiring manager will likely perceive that gap.

---

### Output 1 — Role Fit Assessment

**The question this answers:** Can this person genuinely do this job?

This is an honest assessment of how well the candidate's background maps to the core requirements of the role. It is not a score for encouragement — it is a clear statement of fit.

The assessment:
- States overall fit as a percentage, calibrated to the essential requirements of the JD
- Breaks down each requirement individually as met, partially met, or a gap
- Names the gaps clearly rather than softening them
- Does not inflate fit to be encouraging

**Why this output exists:** Candidates routinely apply to roles for which they are not competitive, either because they have not read the JD carefully or because they have overestimated how well their experience maps. An honest fit assessment before investing a full tailoring run saves significant time — and prevents the worse outcome of reaching interview stage underqualified.

**Scoring guidance:**
- 70%+ = STRONG or COMPETITIVE — proceed with full tailoring
- 40–69% = MARGINAL — proceed with a cover letter strategy that addresses the gaps directly
- Below 40% = WEAK — reconsider before investing a full rewrite

---

### Output 2 — Seniority Calibration

**The question this answers:** Does this CV read at the right level for this role?

Seniority is not just about years of experience — it is about how experience is described. A mid-level CV and a senior CV can contain identical facts but present them entirely differently.

This output assesses:
- Whether bullets describe tasks or outcomes (tasks signal junior; outcomes signal senior)
- Whether the scope of impact matches the role level (managing a team of two reads differently from managing a team of twelve)
- Whether the language reflects ownership and decision-making or contribution and execution
- Whether the profile positions the candidate at the right level for the specific role

**Why this output exists:** Seniority mismatch is one of the most common reasons technically qualified candidates are screened out. A senior role requires evidence of senior-level thinking — not just senior-level experience. Candidates who have been doing senior work but describing it in junior language are routinely underestimated by initial screeners.

---

### Output 3 — Keyword & Positioning Gap Analysis

**The question this answers:** What is missing, and what is being called something different?

This output has three components:

**Present:** Keywords and concepts from the JD that appear in the CV — these confirm coverage and can be flagged as strengths.

**Missing:** Keywords and concepts from the JD that do not appear in the CV at all — these are genuine gaps that need addressing or honest acknowledgement.

**Framing mismatches:** Concepts the candidate clearly has experience in, but describes using different terminology to the JD. This is the most actionable category — it requires no new experience, only a language adjustment.

**Why this output exists:** ATS systems and human reviewers both scan for specific terms. A candidate who has done the work but called it something different from what the JD asks for will be screened out by a system that cannot infer synonyms. Identifying the framing gap is more valuable than identifying the knowledge gap — because framing mismatches can be fixed without inventing experience.

The AI engine that powers this output understands semantic equivalence — it knows that 'Logic Apps and Playbooks' and 'SOAR' describe the same class of capability. This is why the framework does not require domain-specific keyword libraries. The AI interprets the terminology gap without being told in advance what the equivalences are.

---

### Output 4 — Expand These Points

**The question this answers:** What is on the CV but not doing enough work for this role?

This output identifies specific bullets or sections that are relevant to the role but are undersold — where more detail, context, or outcome would significantly strengthen the application.

The distinction from Output 3 is important: Output 3 flags what is missing. Output 4 flags what is present but insufficient. These require different responses.

For each flagged point, the output:
- Quotes or references the specific CV content
- Explains why it matters for this specific role
- Identifies what additional detail would strengthen it

**What this output does not do:** It does not rewrite the bullet. That is the candidate's job. The framework identifies the gap; the candidate — who knows what they actually did — fills it.

**Why this output exists:** Candidates routinely undersell their most relevant experience because they are writing for a general audience rather than for a specific role. A bullet written for a previous application may be technically accurate but fail to surface the detail that would make it compelling for this one.

---

### Output 5 — Interview Risk Flags

**The question this answers:** What will a hiring manager probe on — and are you ready?

This output identifies aspects of the CV that are likely to generate probing questions in interview: gaps, transitions, claims that need substantiation, experience that looks thin relative to the role's requirements, or anything that a hiring manager who has interviewed for this role before would notice.

For each flag, the output:
- Names the risk clearly
- Explains what a hiring manager is likely to ask
- Rates the risk as high, medium, or low based on how central the gap is to the role

**Why this output exists:** Getting to interview with the wrong CV is worse than not getting to interview at all. A candidate who reaches interview with claims they cannot defend damages their credibility in a way that a rejected application does not. Interview risk flags turn the CV analysis into interview preparation — which is a more honest use of the exercise than producing a document that looks good but falls apart under questioning.

## Part 5 — CV Format Specification

Formatting decisions affect whether your CV can be read by an ATS, whether it passes a six-second recruiter scan, and whether it communicates the right level of professionalism. These are not stylistic preferences — they have measurable effects on whether your application reaches human review.

### Page layout

- **Two A4 pages** is the UK standard for 5–10 years' experience. One page if under three years, up to three pages only for academic or very senior roles with extensive publication/programme history. 91% of UK recruiters cite two pages as ideal (Reed.co.uk).
- **Single column throughout.** Two-column layouts increase ATS parse failure from 4% to 14% (.docx) or 18% to 28% (PDF). The efficiency gain is not worth the risk.
- **No tables, text boxes, or images.** These break parsing in Taleo, Workday, and Greenhouse.
- **No content in headers or footers.** ATS systems frequently ignore header and footer content entirely. TopResume found 25% of contact details in headers/footers are dropped.

### Contact header

In the body of the document, not in the header. Include: full name, location (city/region only — not full address), phone, professional email, LinkedIn URL. Optionally: GitHub, portfolio, clearance status.

Do not include: date of birth, photograph, nationality, marital status. This is current UK professional practice under Equality Act 2010 guidance.

### Experience block structure

For each role:
```
Job Title | Company Name | Month Year – Month Year (or Present)
```
- Reverse chronological — most recent first
- Date format: `February 2023 – Present` consistently. En-dash, not hyphen.
- 4–6 bullets per role for recent positions, 2–3 for older ones
- Each bullet opens with an action verb
- Each bullet contains an observable or measurable outcome where one genuinely exists

### Bold usage rules

Bold is a navigational tool, not an emphasis tool. Use it for: job titles, company names, section headers. Do not bold random phrases within bullets — it reads as an AI tell and breaks reading flow.

### UK-specific conventions

- **No personal pronouns.** Implied first person is UK convention. Never write I, me, or my in a CV.
- **British English throughout.** Organisation, Defence, Analyse, Programme. American spelling signals AI generation or inattention — both damaging in technical roles.
- **Reverse chronological is the gold standard.** Functional CVs are viewed with significant suspicion by UK recruiters and should only be used with explicit justification.

### ATS systems in the UK market

| System | Notes |
|---|---|
| Oracle Taleo | Widely used in large enterprise and public sector. Does not resolve acronyms. Always expand on first use. |
| Workday Recruiting | Common in financial services. Handles .docx well. Multi-column layouts frequently break. |
| Greenhouse | Common in tech. Better parsing than Taleo but still affected by tables and columns. |
| Lever | Common in tech. Handles modern .docx well but drops header/footer content. |
